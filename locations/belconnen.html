Non-Destructive Digging Across Belconnen

Belconnen is one of Canberra's largest and most established districts, developed predominantly through the 1970s and 1980s. The infrastructure laid during that era — water and sewer mains, gas distribution networks, early telecommunications conduits — is now approaching or past its expected service life, and the asset records from that period are frequently incomplete by today's standards.

For trades working in Belconnen, this creates a consistent and well-understood risk: DBYD plans may show approximate service corridors, but exact positions, depths, and the presence of privately installed infrastructure often cannot be confirmed from plans alone. Non-destructive digging before mechanical excavation is the practical way to manage that risk — and GreenVac's compact rig covers all of Belconnen's suburbs.

The Challenge of Aging Infrastructure

Water and sewer mains installed under ACT government standards of the 1970s were typically laid at depths that reflected the excavation equipment of the era — not necessarily consistent depths, and often shallower than current codes would require. Seasonal ground movement in Canberra's clay-heavy soils can shift services further over decades. The result is infrastructure that may be 200mm shallower than a DBYD plan suggests, or that has drifted laterally from its recorded position.

This is particularly relevant in suburbs like Macquarie, Page, Latham, and Evatt, where original estate infrastructure is now 50+ years old. Infill development — new extensions, granny flats, pool installations, and connection works — is commonplace across Belconnen, and every new dig in these established streets carries the risk of encountering something unexpected.

Belconnen also includes the University of Canberra campus and the associated Bruce precinct, where a mix of institutional, commercial, and residential infrastructure creates a complex underground environment. Potholing before any significant ground works in this area is especially important.
